Let's dive into some of the juicy logic stuff! What you're learning here is most useful when you have the arguments you want to look at in writing. But, the more you get used to this way of looking at arguments, the easier it gets to use this knowledge on the fly, too. We'll start with two short arguments. Try to spot the difference:

(P1) When I smoke, I have a bad consciousness.
(P2) I smoke.
(C) I have a bad consciousness.

(P1) When a storm is coming, the barometer falls.
(P2) The barometer isn't falling.
(C) There won't be a storm.

The devil is in the detail here. Or rather, the logic is in the structure. The way the conclusion is supported by the premises differs. There are, of course, fancy latin words for each structure. The first one is called a Modus Ponens and the second one a Modus Tollens. You don't need to remember these terms if you don't plan on becoming a good philosopher but it's important to understand these structures to spot bad arguments.

To understand the Modus Ponens, look at the following argument. As you might have picked up in the previous lessons, we use P to indicate that something is a premise and C to indicate a conclusion.

(P1) If Ludwig is fluent in Spanish, he understands the word "cajón".
(P2) Ludwig is fluent in Spanish.
(C) Ludwig understands the word "cajón".

The fun thing about logic as a way of understanding arguments is that the content doesn't matter! We're only interested in the structure of the argument. You'll soon understand why. The structure of this argument can be broken down to:

p -> q
p
q

Yeah ... let's see why. The idea is to replace each individual proposition with a letter. So "If Ludwig is fluent in Spanish, he understands the word "cajón"." becomes p and "Ludwig is fluent in Spanish." becomes q. The arrow indicates a if then relationship. So if p is the case then q follows from that. So if p is the case, you can only conclude that q is also the case. Notice that the only thing we do is replace the propositions with letters. The structure stays the same and becomes more visible. So what if we switch the letters?

p -> q

q

p

Looks fine? Nah, just plug in the propositions again and you'll see why this doesn't make a good argument:

(P1) If Ludwig is fluent in Spanish, he understands the word "cajón".

(P2) Ludwig understands the word "cajón".

(K) Ludwig is fluent in Spanish.

This argument suggests that if Ludwig knows the word "cajón", he must be fluent in Spanish. However, just because someone understands one word in a language, it doesn't necessarily mean they're fluent in that language. For instance, I can say "I love you" in several languages, but that doesn't mean I'm fluent or even understand anything else in those languages.

This type of error in reasoning is called a (logical) fallacy. If an argument follows this structure, it's never valid, and you don't even need to look at the content of the argument to know this. It might seem strange at first, but don't worry; you'll get the hang of it soon!

To better understand fallacies, let's add some more terminology to your repertoire. In this argument, if Ludwig didn't understand the word "cajón", he wouldn't be fluent in Spanish. This means that understanding the word "cajón" is a necessary condition for fluency in Spanish. However, understanding just one word isn't sufficient to be considered fluent.

A necessary condition is a condition that must be present for something else to occur or be true. In contrast, a sufficient condition is a condition that, if met, guarantees that something else will occur or be true. Together, a necessary and sufficient condition means that if and only if the condition is met, the related event or statement will occur or be true.

Understanding these terms will help you identify and avoid logical fallacies in arguments.

We'll need the same terms to understand the structure of a Modus Tollens. This structure works with negation:

(P1) If Ludwig is fluent in Spanish, he understands the word "cajón". p → q
(P2) Ludwig does not understand the word "cajón". ¬q
(C) Ludwig is not fluent in Spanish. ¬p

Ludwig doesn't understand the word "cajón", but since this is a necessary condition to be fluent in Spanish, he isn't fluent in Spanish. This is a valid logical structure. Generally, the if then condition always works the same way. It always gives a necessary but not sufficient condition for the if-part to be true.

Consider the modified argument:

(P1) Ludwig is fluent in Spanish if and only if he can both understand and speak the language effectively. p ↔ q
(P2) Ludwig can both understand and speak Spanish effectively. q
(C) Ludwig is fluent in Spanish. p

In this argument, the "if and only if" condition establishes that Ludwig's ability to understand and speak Spanish effectively is both necessary and sufficient for fluency in the language. Necessary, because without these skills, Ludwig cannot be considered fluent; sufficient, because having these skills guarantees Ludwig's fluency in Spanish.

Breaking down the "if and only if" condition:

  1. If Ludwig can understand and speak Spanish effectively, then he is fluent in Spanish (sufficient condition).
  2. Only if Ludwig is fluent in Spanish can he understand and speak the language effectively (necessary condition).

So in this case, the "if and only if" condition ensures that the conclusion (Ludwig's fluency in Spanish) is both necessary and sufficient, given the premise (Ludwig's ability to understand and speak Spanish effectively).

How about one last structure to give you a solid foundation?

(P1) The Earth is a disc.
(P2) If the Earth is a disc, then people fall off its edge. p → q
(P3) People do not fall off the edge. ¬q
(C) The Earth is not a disc. ¬p

This argument sets up a cause-and-effect relationship between the shape of the Earth and the expected consequence of people falling off its edge. Since we observe that people do not fall off the edge of the Earth (P3), it logically follows that the Earth cannot be a disc (C), given the premise (P2). We call this structure a reductio ad absurdum, which means reduction to absurdity.

In a reductio ad absurdum, you assume the truth of a premise and then show that it leads to an absurd or contradictory conclusion, thus demonstrating that the initial premise must be false.

Let's analyze the argument again, highlighting its reductio ad absurdum structure:

  1. Assume the premise that the Earth is a disc (P1).
  2. If the Earth is a disc, then it logically follows that people should fall off its edge (P2).
  3. However, we observe that people do not fall off the edge of the Earth (P3), which contradicts the expected consequence of a disc-shaped Earth.
  4. Since the consequence is false, the initial premise (P1) that the Earth is a disc must also be false.

Thus, this argument effectively employs reductio ad absurdum to challenge the notion of a disc-shaped Earth by demonstrating an absurd or contradictory outcome. The structure of a reductio ad absurdum looks like this (in this case):

p
p → q
¬q
¬p

Now, there are many more logical structures. If you want to dive deep into this topic, it's worth learning about them. But for casually analyzing your and others' arguments, y ou already have a good foundation. Still, you might ask yourself, "Why bother looking at the structure?" There is one simple reason: arguments in the real world get complicated. They contain more than just two or three premises; there are hidden premises you've got to make explicit to understand the argument, and truly understanding the content gets hard. Therefore, we start with figuring out the structure of an argument and just see whether it's valid. Because if not, we don't even have to bother with fact-checking it. And if it is valid, we have already built a better understanding of the argument, and it's easier to understand the content and see whether the facts are factual or fiction.

To end this section, we'll look at a real-life example. This should give you an idea of the complexity of arguments, although this example isn't even too complicated:

WALLACE: Gentlemen, a lot of people have been waiting for this night, so let’s get going. Our first subject is the Supreme Court. President Trump, you nominated Amy Coney Barrett over the weekend to succeed the late Ruth Bader Ginsburg on the Court. My first question to both of you tonight, why are you right in the argument you make and your opponent wrong? And where do you think a Justice Barrett would take the court? President Trump, in this first segment, you go first. Two minutes.

TRUMP: Thank you very much, Chris. I will tell you very simply. We won the election. Elections have consequences. We have the Senate, we have the White House, and we have a phenomenal nominee respected by all. Top, top academic, good in every way. Good in every way. In fact, some of her biggest endorsers are very liberal people from Notre Dame and other places. So I think she’s going to be fantastic. We have plenty of time. Even if we did it after the election itself. I have a lot of time after the election, as you know. So I think that she will be outstanding. The only difference is they’d try and do it faster. There’s no way they would give it up. They had Merrick Garland, but the problem is they didn’t have the election so they were stopped. This segment from the presidential debate between Donald Trump and Joe Biden in 2020 demonstrates a complex argument involving the nomination of Amy Coney Barrett to the Supreme Court. What structure do we have here? Yes, none! Not so neatly as in the examples before, at least. We can indeed make out a modus ponens here, but would have to rearrange things:

  1. (P1) If you win the election, you control the Senate and the White House.
  2. (P2) If you control the Senate and the White House, you have the right to choose and confirm a Supreme Court Justice.
  3. (P3) The Republicans (and Donald Trump) won the election.
  4. (C1) The Republicans have the right to choose and confirm a Supreme Court Justice.
  5. (P4) A good Supreme Court Justice is highly qualified and respected.
  6. (P5) Amy Coney Barrett is a highly qualified nominee, respected by people across the political spectrum.
  7. (C2) Amy Coney Barrett makes a good Supreme Court Justice.
  8. (P6) It is not unprecedented or unconstitutional to confirm a Supreme Court nominee during an election year when the same political party controls the presidency and the Senate.
  9. (P7) Democrats would have proceeded with the nomination of Merrick Garland if they had controlled the Senate in 2016.
  10. (P8) The Republicans control the presidency and the Senate. (Equal to P2)
  11. (C3) The Republicans and Trump have the right to choose and confirm Amy Coney Barrett for the Supreme Court.

This is just one way to reconstruct the argument. You could do it differently. The important part is to stick to the original as closely as possible and decide on which features of the argument you want to highlight. It's good practice to make arguments as strong as possible, even if you don't agree and want to tear it down. Because the stronger an opponent's argument, the more weight does your counterargument have. That's why I've tried my best to tickle out a strong logical structure that is easy to understand. Whether the argument holds up now depends on its soundness because the logical structure looks good. All the propositions that start with (P) could be right or wrong. The argument fails as soon as you can argue for the wrongness of one of the premises.
