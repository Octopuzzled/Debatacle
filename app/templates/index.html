Why making sense of debates is an essential skill

Imagine you're having a discussion about something that is important to you. You have an opinion, of course. But the other person has a different one. What do you do? If you don't tend to just scream at the person, you instead explain why you have this opinion and why you think it's right or at least a good one. Maybe you can convince the other person, maybe they convince you. But how do you make your point as strong as possible instead of rambling?

Or imagine you've just heard politicians tell the public why they're awesome and their plans are good. How do you know if you agree or not? You could just believe the person who looks better or uses shorter sentences because that's easy to understand. Or you could figure out whether what they're saying makes sense in the first place. But how do you go about that?

Congratulations, you've come to a place that is designed to help you make sense of the chaos that emerges when people talk. After all, complex communication is part of the reason why humanity has evolved so much. We figure things out in dialogues. But while communication comes fairly natural to us, the more complex the topic, the less natural does understanding come. That's what we need logic for.

We need to give arguments for our opinions and logic is the teaching of how to make good arguments.

Don't worry though, it's not that difficult. I'll provide you with all the basics to get started on your journey.
